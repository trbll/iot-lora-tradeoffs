What is Spreading Factor?

LoRa sends data using chirpsA chirp is a signal whose frequency sweeps smoothly from low to high (or high to low) over a fixed time. Think of a bird chirp rising in pitch — same idea, just in radio waves. — radio signals that sweep up in frequency. The Spreading FactorSpreading Factor (SF7–SF12) sets how many chirps are used to send each piece of data. More chirps = slower but tougher signal. SF7 uses 128 chirps per symbol, SF12 uses 4,096. controls how much each chirp is "spread out" in time. A higher SF stretches each transmission longer, which makes it harder to lose in noiseMore chirps per symbol means more redundancy. The receiver can average over a longer pattern, filtering out random noise. This is called processing gain — each extra SF step adds ~2.5 dB of sensitivity. and receivable at greater distancesThe radio waves don't actually travel farther — higher SF gives more processing gain, meaning the receiver can decode a much weaker signal. The range increase comes from better sensitivity, not stronger transmission., but sends data more slowlyAt SF7 you get ~5,470 bits/sec. At SF12 it drops to ~250 bits/sec — roughly 22× slower. Each SF step roughly halves the data rate because each symbol takes twice as long. and keeps the radio on longer (more time on airTime on Air is literally how long the radio waves are being transmitted for a single message. Longer time on air = more battery drain and more spectrum usage.). Each step up in SF roughly doubles the time on airBecause each SF adds one bit to the symbol, the symbol length doubles (2^SF). SF7 symbol = 1.02 ms, SF8 = 2.05 ms, SF9 = 4.10 ms, and so on. A 10-byte message takes ~39 ms at SF7 but ~991 ms at SF12.. The formula is T_sym = 2^SF / BWBandwidth (BW) is how wide the frequency range is that the chirp sweeps across. Wider = faster but shorter range. Common values: 125 kHz and 500 kHz..

Hear the Spreading Factor

Pick a spreading factor and listen to the same message at different speeds. At SF7 it plays fast — like someone talking quickly in a noisy room, easy to miss. At SF12 it's stretched 32× slower — like that same person repeating themselves very deliberately, giving your ears much more time to catch every word. The content is identical; only the delivery speed changes.

Transmission Lifecycle

Every LoRaWAN uplink goes through 11 statesWake up, radio prep, transmit, wait for RX1, listen on RX1, wait for RX2, listen on RX2, radio off, post-processing, shutdown sequence, and sleep. Each state draws a different amount of current. — from waking the radio to returning to sleep. Most of the energy is spent during the brief TX burstThe transmit phase is when the radio is actively sending your data over the air. It draws the most current (~83 mA) and its duration depends directly on your SF and payload size. and the two receive windowsRX1 opens 1 second after TX ends, RX2 opens 1 second after RX1. The device briefly listens for a downlink response from the gateway (e.g., an ACK or configuration change). If nothing is heard, the windows close and the device goes back to sleep. (RX1 & RX2) where the device listens for a downlink from the gateway. The current-draw graph below shows the power profile of a single transmission cycle. Higher SF = longer TX phase = more energy per message.

Sleep vs. Active Power Draw

LoRa devices spend 99%+ of their time sleepingA device transmitting once per hour with SF7 is active for only ~2 seconds out of 3,600. That's a duty cycle of ~0.05%. The rest of the time the radio is off, drawing almost no power.. The difference between sleep current and transmit current spans orders of magnitudeSleep current is ~0.045 mA (45 microamps). Transmit current is ~83 mA. That's a ratio of roughly 1,844× — almost four orders of magnitude. This enormous gap is what makes multi-year battery life possible. — this is why LoRa devices can run for years on a coin cell. Based on Multitech mDot / SX1272 measurements from Casals et al., Sensors 2017.

Battery Life Calculator

How long will your device last on a battery? It depends on the duty cycleDuty Cycle (D) is the fraction of time the device is actively transmitting vs. sleeping. D = Active Time / (Active Time + Sleep Time). A device transmitting once per hour has a very low duty cycle. — the ratio of active time to sleep time. Since sleep current is ~2000× lower than transmit current, how often you sendSending every 1 minute vs. every 60 minutes is a 60× difference in battery life. The transmit interval is the single biggest lever for extending device lifetime. matters far more than how much you sendSay your device sends 10 bytes at SF7 — that's ~39 ms of TX time. Doubling to 20 bytes might add only ~20 ms more. But going from sending every 60 minutes to every 30 minutes doubles the number of full TX cycles (wake, prep, TX, RX windows, sleep) — each costing ~2 seconds of active time. The overhead of waking the radio dominates the cost of the actual payload..
